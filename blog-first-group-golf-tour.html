What to Expect on Your First Group Golf Tour to Asia

By Dean Page 1 February 2026 6 min read

If you've been thinking about doing a golf trip to Thailand or Vietnam but haven't quite committed, chances are you're wondering what it's actually like. Not the brochure version - the real version. What do the days look like? What happens when you arrive? How does the group dynamic work? And will you feel like you're on a tour bus with strangers, or will it feel like a proper trip?

I've been running group golf tours to Asia for years, and I hear these questions all the time. So here's an honest account of what to expect, from wheels-up to wheels-down.

The Arrival

On a well-organised tour, you won't be left to figure things out at the airport. There will be a driver or transfer vehicle meeting you at arrivals, and if you're arriving from Australia you'll likely land in Thailand late at night. Your hotel will be booked, check-in will be smooth and your welcome briefing will either happen that evening or first thing the next morning.

That first briefing covers your tee times, course schedule, transport arrangements, any social dinners and what to do if you need help. It takes about 20 minutes and it means you have the full picture before you tee off on day one.

A Typical Golf Day

Most golf courses in Thailand want you on the first tee between 7am and 8am. The heat builds through the day, and playing in the cool of the morning is genuinely more enjoyable. That means breakfast around 6:15am, transfer to the course by 6:45am and arriving at the clubhouse with time to hit a few on the range.

You'll be assigned your caddie at the pro shop. On most courses in Pattaya and Phuket, caddies are mandatory. By the 4th or 5th hole, your caddie will know your game, know how far you hit each club and be reading greens for you with real accuracy. It's one of the genuine highlights of golf in Asia.

The round itself takes roughly four to four and a half hours. After 18 holes you'll head back to the clubhouse for lunch, cold drinks and the inevitable scoring debrief. Transfers back to the hotel are usually arranged for after lunch, which means you're back by early to mid-afternoon.

Afternoons and Evenings

Afternoons are yours. Some blokes nap. Some explore. Some sit by the pool. There's no obligation to be anywhere until dinner, which is usually a group meal at a good local restaurant. These dinners are where the real camaraderie builds. You'll swap stories about the round, give each other grief about that three-putt on 14 and start looking forward to tomorrow.

Rest days are built into longer tours. They're not filler - they're useful. You might visit a local market, organise a spa afternoon, or just use the day to recover for the next round. Nobody is expected to be on the go every minute.

The Group Dynamic

This is the question I get most often. "What if I don't know anyone?" The honest answer is that by the end of day two, it won't matter. Golf is an excellent leveller. Playing 72 holes alongside someone creates a kind of shared experience that accelerates friendships in a way that's hard to replicate elsewhere.

The groups we run tend to be Australian men in the 50 to 70 age bracket who love their golf and aren't interested in a resort holiday where golf is an afterthought. They're serious about their game, relaxed about the social side of things and genuinely good company. First-timers are always welcomed by the regulars.

What's Included and What's Not

On a Prestige Golf Tours trip, your package includes accommodation, all green fees, cart or caddie fees, group transfers between hotel and courses, and most evening meals. What you cover yourself is personal items, alcohol, flights to and from Australia, and anything outside the scheduled activities. It's a clean split and there are no nasty surprises.

For anyone considering the Pattaya tour or the Phuket Paradise package, the course schedule and inclusions are spelled out clearly before you book, and I'm happy to walk through any of it over email.

One Last Thing

The most common piece of feedback I get from first-timers is a variation of: "I wish I'd done this sooner." Not because it's luxury beyond belief, but because the combination of great golf, warm weather, good company and a total change of scene is genuinely hard to replicate any other way. It's the kind of trip that resets you.
