Caddie Culture in Thailand: What Every Australian Golfer Needs to Know

1 August 2025 By Dean Page 7 min read

One of the biggest surprises for Australians playing golf in Thailand for the first time is the caddie system. Back home, caddies are something you might see at a professional event or a very exclusive private club. In Thailand, they're part of every round at every decent course, caddies are compulsory, and within about three holes you'll wonder how you ever played without one.

But there are things you need to know before you tee off. Getting the most from your Thai caddie is partly about understanding what they do, and partly about approaching the relationship with the right attitude.

What a Thai Caddie Actually Does

This is not the Australia-style arrangement where a mate offers to carry your bag for a walk. A Thai caddie is a trained, experienced golf professional in their own right. Here's what you can expect:

  • Carry your bag the entire round, typically on a strap rather than a cart. Some courses use trolleys depending on terrain.
  • Read your putts. This is serious. They know the greens better than any GPS device because they've walked them every week for years. Most caddies will stand behind your ball, assess the break, and tell you left edge, right edge, or straight with a high degree of accuracy.
  • Club selection advice. They'll give you a distance, factor in wind, and often suggest a club. You're not obliged to take it, but on an unfamiliar course, it's usually worth listening.
  • Clean your clubs between shots, rake bunkers, replace divots, tend the flag, and hand you water from the caddie trolley.
  • Keep your scorecard. They track your score and keep things moving.
  • Help find errant shots in the rough or trees.

In practical terms, your caddie removes virtually every administrative burden from your round. You can focus entirely on hitting the ball well. It's a genuinely better way to play golf.

Tipping Etiquette: What You Should Pay

Caddies earn a base wage from the course, but their tips make up the majority of their income. The accepted tip range at most courses in Thailand is 300 to 500 baht. At the high-end courses - Siam Country Club, Blue Canyon, Nikanti - 500 baht is the right number and you won't regret paying it. At smaller or resort courses, 300 to 400 baht is appropriate.

If your caddie was exceptional - accurate reads, positive energy, found your ball in an impossibly dense patch of rough, kept you laughing for four hours - tip 600 baht. In Australian dollars at current rates, the difference between 300 and 600 baht is about $12. Tip generously.

Always tip in cash, in Thai baht, directly to your caddie at the end of the round. Don't put it on a card or leave it at the pro shop unless the course specifically asks you to.

What Caddies Are Paid (And Why This Matters)

Understanding the economics helps. A caddie's base wage from the course is often relatively modest - perhaps 200 to 300 baht per round from the club itself, depending on the course. Their tips from golfers are where the real income comes from. Most caddies are women from rural backgrounds who are supporting families. The work is physically demanding - carrying bags in 30+ degree heat for four hours - and they do it with remarkable grace and positivity.

Approaching your caddie with respect isn't just the right thing to do, it changes the entire dynamic of the round. Golfers who treat their caddie well, learn a few Thai words, and show appreciation get better information, more encouragement, and a more enjoyable experience. It's not complicated.

Communication Tips

Most caddies at the major courses speak enough English to communicate club recommendations and course information. Some are highly conversational. A few basics will help:

  • "How far?" is universally understood and will get you a distance in metres.
  • "Which way?" works for reading breaks on the green.
  • "Sawadee krap" (if you're male) or "Sawadee kha" (if you're female) - the Thai greeting - will always get a smile.
  • "Kob khun krap/kha" - thank you - delivered at the end of the round with your tip is a small gesture that lands well every time.

Don't worry if conversation is limited. A caddie who doesn't speak much English will still communicate everything important through pointing, gesturing, and club selection. The job gets done regardless.

Common Mistakes Australians Make

A few things that occasionally create awkwardness:

  • Ignoring the putt read. Some golfers take one look at a putt, decide they know better, and play straight when their caddie told them it breaks right. The caddie is usually right. At least listen before dismissing.
  • Carrying the bag yourself. Some golfers have a reflex to grab their own bag or reach for their own clubs. Let your caddie do their job - it's what they're there for and it can feel dismissive when you take over.
  • Forgetting the tip until reminded. Have 500 baht set aside in your pocket before you reach the 18th green. Don't be the person scrambling for cash at the bag drop.
  • Being impatient or short-tempered. If you miss a putt, that's not your caddie's fault. Keep the energy positive. A happy caddie is a helpful caddie.

The Bottom Line

The caddie experience in Thailand is one of the genuine highlights of playing golf in Asia. It's not just a novelty - having a knowledgeable local guide walking every hole with you, caring about your score, and making the round flow easily is simply a better way to experience a golf course. Most Australian golfers come back from their first Thai trip wishing they could import the system.
