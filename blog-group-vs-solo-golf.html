Why Group Golf Tours Beat Solo Trips Every Time

1 July 2025 By Dean Page 7 min read

Plenty of experienced travellers assume that organising their own golf trip to Thailand will give them more freedom and a better deal. And there's something to that instinct - nobody likes being herded around on a bus schedule that doesn't suit them. But when you actually sit down and map out what a self-organised golf trip to Southeast Asia involves, the calculus usually shifts pretty quickly in favour of a well-run group tour. Here's why.

The Cost Savings Are Real

Golf courses in Thailand, Vietnam, and the Philippines operate on a tiered pricing system. Individual bookings pay rack rate. Tour operators who bring consistent groups of golfers to the same courses over years negotiate better rates. That saving gets passed on to you.

The same applies to accommodation. Group bookings at quality hotels attract group rates that are meaningfully lower than the best individual rate you'll find on any booking platform. When you factor in the airport transfers, internal transfers between courses, and any group meals that are included, the package deal quite often works out cheaper than booking everything yourself - and you haven't had to spend 15 hours on the phone and online making it happen.

There's also the hidden cost of mistakes. When you're organising a golf trip solo in a country where you don't speak the language and don't know which tee times are busiest, which hotels are actually close to the courses, and which courses are worth the premium green fee, you're going to make some expensive errors. Group tours eliminate that learning curve.

The Social Experience Is the Point

Let's be honest: a significant part of why Australians do golf trips is the social side. The dinners, the 19th hole stories, the wagers on the back nine, the group WhatsApp thread that's still active two years later. Solo trips to Asia are fine but they're a fundamentally different experience.

On a group tour with 10 to 16 like-minded golfers, you're sharing every moment of the trip with people who care about the same things you do. The round on day two becomes the reference point for every conversation on day three. Someone holes out from 80 metres on the par four at Laem Chabang and that shot is being retold at dinner for the rest of the week. These shared experiences are what make a golf trip memorable rather than just enjoyable.

Plenty of golfers who join Prestige Golf Tours as solo travellers - slotting into a group where they know nobody - come home with people they consider genuine mates. That's not an accident. When you spend five days on the course with people who share your passion for the game, connections happen naturally.

You Don't Have to Organise Anything

This sounds trivial until you've tried to book a tee time at Siam Country Club from a laptop in Melbourne while managing the time difference, figuring out the deposit process, checking whether your group qualifies for member pricing, and then cross-referencing it with your hotel's proximity and transfer timing. It's not simple.

Organising a five-day golf trip to Thailand involves: flights, airport transfers, hotel bookings, tee time bookings at four or five different courses, caddie confirmations, internal transfers between venues, restaurant bookings, and a backup plan when something changes. That's a part-time job for a few weeks. On a tour, someone else has already done all of it - and done it better than you could, because they've done it dozens of times before.

Safety and Peace of Mind

Travelling in groups is simply safer, particularly in a country you're visiting for the first time. You have people around you, someone who knows where you are, and an operator who has local contacts and established protocols if something goes wrong. Medical situations, lost property, flight changes - a tour operator handles these things. A solo traveller handles them alone, in a foreign country, possibly in the middle of the night.

That peace of mind has a value that's hard to quantify until you need it.

The Difference Between a Great Group Tour and a Cattle Run

It's fair to say that not all group tours are equal. There are large-scale operations that move 40 or 50 people through destinations on a rigid schedule, with no flexibility, mediocre courses, and an atmosphere more like a school excursion than a mates' golf trip. That's not what a good group tour looks like.

The best group golf tours keep numbers small. Twelve to sixteen golfers is a sweet spot - big enough to fill a golf day with proper competition, small enough to move efficiently and keep the social group tight. The itinerary should be designed for golfers, not people who happen to be playing golf on the side. Courses should be genuine quality, not just whoever gives the best bulk discount. And the pace of the trip should allow time for the things that make it memorable, not just tick boxes.
